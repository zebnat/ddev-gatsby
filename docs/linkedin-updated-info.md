An engineer at Anthropic wrote a spec, pointed Claude at an Asana board, and went home. Claude did the rest.

Claude broke the spec into tickets, spawned agents for each one, and they started building independently. When an agent got confused, it ran git-blame and messaged the right engineer in Slack.

By Monday, the agents had finished the feature.

This is the part that fascinates me most: the agent's learning loop mirrors how a new team members onboards. It reads the codebase, identifies who knows what, and asks the right humans. Slack and email - that's all it needed.

We're approaching a world where developers orchestrate dozens of AI agents in parallel. And when that happens, the differentiator between a good engineer and a great one comes down to one thing:

The quality of your specs.

You can't write a spec that holds up at that scale without genuinely understanding what you're building at a deeper level. Architecture, edge cases, failure modes, integration points. The spec IS the product thinking.

Having agents run over the weekend to ship work will start to become entirely normal. There's zero reason you wouldn't add two extra days of output with a couple of prompts and a machine running in the background.

That said - I'm always a little skeptical of how viable these patterns broadly are but do think this is the direction we're heading in. Verification remains the biggest unsolved challenge. Tests that pass on the pre-patch codebase, UI verification, pathological coding practices leaking into the context window. We need slightly better models and much better evaluation infrastructure for this to become the default. I believe we will get there.

But the trajectory is clear: the next-gen developer who understands fundamentals, can architect well, and knows how to orchestrate agents is going to be a force-multiplier.

My biggest takeaways from Boris Cherny (Head of Claude Code):

1. Coding is now “solved” for most use cases. Boris hasn’t written a single line of code by hand since November, with 100% of his work now authored by Claude Code. At the same time, he remains one of the most productive engineers at Anthropic, shipping 10 to 30 pull requests daily while leading the team.

2. Anthropic has seen a 200% increase in engineer productivity since adopting Claude Code. As Boris notes, “Back at Meta, with hundreds of engineers working on productivity, we’d see gains of a few percentage points in a year. Now we’re seeing hundreds of percentage points.”

3. AI is moving beyond writing code to generating ideas. “Claude is starting to come up with ideas. It’s looking through feedback, bug reports, and telemetry, then suggesting features to ship.”

4. The next roles to be transformed are those adjacent to engineering. Product managers, designers, and data scientists will see similar transformations as agentic AI expands beyond coding. “Any kind of job where you use computer tools will be next.”

5. Build for the model six months from now, not today. One of Boris’s key principles is to design products for future AI capabilities, not current ones. “It’s going to be uncomfortable because your product-market fit won’t be very good for the first six months. But when that model comes out, you’ll hit the ground running.”

6. Watch for “latent demand.” Claude Code was built by observing what people were already trying to do, and then making it easier. Cowork emerged when they noticed people using Claude Code for non-coding tasks like analyzing MRIs or recovering wedding photos from corrupted drives.

7. Don’t optimize for token cost. Boris advises companies to give engineers unlimited tokens during experimentation phases. “At small scale, the token cost is still relatively low compared to their salary. If an idea works and scales, that’s when you optimize it.”

8. Underfund headcount on purpose. When Boris puts one engineer on a project, they’re forced to let AI do more of the work. Constraint drives creative use of AI tooling, not just faster typing.

9. The most successful people in the future will be generalists. “Try to be a generalist more than you have in the past. Some of the most effective engineers cross over disciplines. The people who will be rewarded most won’t just be AI-native—they’ll be curious generalists who can think about the broader problem they’re solving.”

10. Always use the most capable model, not the cheapest. A less intelligent model often burns more tokens correcting mistakes than a smarter one spends getting it right the first time. Boris runs maximum effort on Opus 4.6 for everything.

Deja de "promptear" y empieza a especificar.

Hay una confusión peligrosa ahí fuera: creer que el Vibe Coding consiste en "tirar prompts y rezar" para que el código funcione.
Si trabajas así, la IA no es tu aliada; es un junior hiperactivo con acceso a producción que va a romper cosas. Tarde o temprano.

La diferencia entre un prototipo que se desmorona y un software escalable tiene nombre: Spec-Driven Development (SDD).

En mis últimos 18 meses trabajando en este flujo, he aprendido que si no sabes diseñar el sistema, la IA no te va a salvar. El SDD es el arte de definir especificaciones tan robustas que la implementación se vuelve una consecuencia lógica, no un experimento de prueba y error.

¿Por qué el SDD es el verdadero "game changer"?
- Fuera alucinaciones: La IA alucina cuando le das libertad creativa sobre la estructura. El SDD le da raíles de acero.
- Contratos antes que código: Definimos las interfaces, los modelos de datos y las reglas de negocio antes de generar una sola línea de lógica.
- La IA como ejecutora, no como arquitecta: Yo diseño la estrategia; la IA ejecuta la táctica. Invertir los roles es la receta perfecta para la deuda técnica técnica infinita.

El Vibe Coding sin una especificación detrás es solo ruido. Con SDD, es la metodología más potente que he usado en mi carrera como ingeniero.

¿Tú cómo lo ves? ¿Estás dedicando más tiempo a escribir prompts o a diseñar las especificaciones de lo que quieres construir?

“Coding agent” has become one of the most misleading phrases in modern engineering.

It is widely misunderstood, often used interchangeably with AI coding methods that do not involve agency, and collapses radically different operating models into a single buzzword that hides the decisions that actually matter.

And in any case, the real question was never whether you should or should not use agents in your product development lifecycle (PDLC).

Instead, you should be asking:

- How much autonomy should AI have in our PDLC?
- Where does accountability stay human?
- What risks are we willing to carry?

AI-assisted development is not binary. It is a spectrum of:

- Manual prompting
- IDE copilots
- Repo-level autonomy
- Deterministic toolchains
- Context-grounded workflows
- Parallelised specialist agents
- Spec-driven development where code becomes commodity

Each step increases leverage, but each step also increases risk, opacity, and governance complexity.

For most product teams, the sweet spot is not “full autonomy”, rather, grounded autonomy:

- AI anchored in team standards and architecture
- Explicit guardrails and conventions
- Humans owning outcomes
- Automation that scales with validation

Push beyond that and most teams begin optimising for the production of code itself, rather than achieving the outcomes the code is there to serve.

At the extreme, spec-driven development flips the model entirely. Specifications become the durable asset. Code becomes interchangeable output. That is not “more agentic” per-se, it is a different operating system for building software.

AI-assisted development is not magic; it operates within, and is bounded by, a socio-technical system. The teams that win will not chase maximum code output. They will design intentional autonomy, clear ownership, and bounded risk as they adopt AI-assisted development.

In my latest piece, I break down the 7 levels of AI-assisted development and how to choose deliberately.

"I really am mostly programming in English now." When the % of code AI writes is as high as 80%, the problems don't disappear, but change.

My latest free deep-dive article: https://lnkd.in/gRG4kMFs ✍ 

Andrej Karpathy recently noted that he is "mostly programming in English now." It's a compelling observation, but it signals a deeper shift than just syntax. When the AI contribution to a codebase is so high (at least for evergreen apps), the friction of software development doesn't disappear, but it migrates.

The nature of the bug has changed. We are no longer chasing syntax errors or logic typos. We are dealing with conceptual failures:

- Agents scaffolding 1,000 lines of complex architecture where 100 lines of script would suffice.

- The AI executing bad architectural decisions without the pushback you'd get from a senior peer. It'll solve for a prompt, but maybe not optimally.

- Wrong assumptions buried deep in the context window.

Whether your % is 80%, 50% or lower, it's likely higher than a year ago. There's a productivity paradox. The data is showing a strange trend. Teams with high AI adoption are merging 98% more PRs, yet review times have spiked by 91%. We have increased the velocity of the car, but the traffic has ground to a halt.

There is also a rise of "comprehension debt" 

This is the metric nobody is tracking, but every lead engineer feels. It is dangerously easy to review code that makes sense in the moment, but that you could not write from scratch. When your ability to read the system doesn't scale with the agent's ability to output code, you stop engineering and start hoping.

We are moving from imperative coding to declarative orchestration. This will inevitably split the field into two distinct groups: those who love the syntax of coding, and those who love the utility of building. The current tooling feels like it may be aggressively optimizing for the latter.

This article a follow-up to last year's thoughts on AI helping you get 70% of the way there. I unpack the data, the "comprehension debt" concept, and the patterns that are actually working in production in my latest post.

😅En Silicon Valley te aprueban con “LGTM”… en Japón te auditan el alma 🧾



Mi primer PR en un equipo japonés tardó cuatro días en fusionarse. No porque rompiera nada, ni porque fuera “malo”, sino porque lo leyeron como si fuera un contrato: intención, riesgos, futuro mantenimiento, decisiones de diseño y casos límite. En Silicon Valley, muchas veces lo mismo se resuelve con tres aprobaciones rápidas y un “pulgar arriba”, priorizando velocidad y aprendizaje por iteración.

La diferencia no es “lento vs rápido”, es “deliberado vs inmediato”. En Japón, la revisión de código también es revisión de arquitectura: preguntan si estás mezclando dominios, si un valor hardcoded se convertirá en deuda, si una decisión se sostendrá en cinco años. Y algo que se siente raro al principio: los comentarios no solo corrigen, enseñan. Buscan que el siguiente desarrollador entienda el porqué, no solo que el CI pase hoy.

¿Quién tiene razón? Ambos, dependiendo del contexto. Si tu producto necesita iteración agresiva, el enfoque del Valle funciona. Si tu sistema debe sobrevivir cambios, rotación y años de mantenimiento, el enfoque japonés es una inversión. Lo importante es ser intencional: revisar como si el sistema importara, dejar comentarios que eleven al equipo y diseñar pensando más allá del sprint.
